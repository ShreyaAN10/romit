Analyzing password strength has been the focus of recent research considering how passwords continue to be the dominant method for authentication. Strong passwords like random strings are challenging to remember, whereas memorable passwords are simple to crack. In this work, we propose a novel approach to generate memorable passphrases for multilingual users.

This user study is being conducted to determine the usability of passphrases created using romanized forms of non-english words and gauge the participants' perception of the security of this approach.

We have set up a dummy signup/login webpage with minimal account registration and login functionalities. We request participants of the study to visit this login page for five days (November 28th – December 2nd) to authenticate themselves at least once per day. While creating passphrases, we request the participants to follow these rules:

  • Rule 1 : Use a passphrase of minimum word count “4”

    A passphrase is a string of words. For instance, twoboysplayingwithball. This passphrase has 5 words: ‘Two’, ‘boys’, ‘playing’, ‘with’, and ‘ball’. Users are requested to use at least 4 words for their passphrases.

  • Rule 2 : Use words from non-English languages to form your passphrases

    Our idea assumes that the participant is at least bilingual or has some means of querying romanizations of non-English words. Please use romanized form of your chosen passphrase for authentication. In Hindi, “Two” is “do”, “boys” is ‘ladke’, ‘playing’ is ‘khelte’, ‘with ball’ is ‘gend ke saath’. Therefore, our example English passphrase ‘twoboysplayingwithball’ when romanised, becomes ‘doladkekheltegendkesath’.
    These phrases need not make sense. They can even be a set of random words. Multilingual participants can use words from more than one non-English language to create a passphrase.
    Example: If a participant knows Hindi, Punjabi, and Gujarati, their passphrase could be ‘Kaise_ho?bdiya@majama’.

  • Rule 3 : Use special characters as separators or leet

    Special characters include characters like “@”, “$”, “%”, “*” etc. Previous studies have proven that using special characters in passphrases makes them more secure and harder to crack. Participants are requested to use at least 3 special characters in their passphrase either as separators or leet.
    Example: ‘Kaise_ho?bdiya@majama’ or ‘d0l@dkekheltegendkes@th’
